Pete Trimmer: Self-Development Drills

Physical practice drills and tactical exercises extracted from Chapter 11: Self-Development in Beyond Expert Croquet (pp. 189-209). Trimmer emphasises practicing what most improves your match-winning odds, not necessarily your weakest skill.

Trimmer's Key Principles

Practice Priority: "Practice whichever skill will most improve your odds of winning matches" — not necessarily your weakest skill.

The Three Key Physical Skills

Trimmer identifies three skills as fundamental (in priority order):

  1. Rushing — most valuable within 5-yard hitting range; approach rushes are critical
  2. Croquet Strokes — three measurable outcomes: angle, relative distance (ratio), and overall power
  3. Controlled Hoop Running — "the skill least well recognised by players at the top level"
"Shooting is demoted to a supplement (Appendix D) as many players put too much time into shooting relative to improving other skills."

Practice Philosophy

  • Combination practice is more valuable than isolated skill practice
  • Mental aspects of each stroke are connected to the next stroke to come
  • Track and measure progress — what gets measured improves
  • Practice under simulated pressure to prepare for matches

Rushing Drills

Source: Trimmer pp. 190-191

Drill Setup & Execution Key Points
Basic Corner Rush Beginner Place rush ball with striker's ball nearby in corner position. Rush to specific hoops:
  • Corner 1 → Hoop 1, then Hoop 4
  • Corner 4 → Hoop 4, then Hoop 1
  • Repeat from corners 2 and 3
 Vary starting positions each session. Focus on approach rushes — getting within 3 yards of target hoop.
Approach Rush Scoring Intermediate Rush ball positioned 7-10 yards from target hoop. Score each rush:
  • 3 points: finishes <1 yard from hoop
  • 2 points: finishes <2 yards
  • 1 point: finishes <3 yards
  • 0 points: >3 yards
 Target: 15+ points from 10 rushes. This is the most valuable rush to practice — 5-yard hitting range makes approach rushes critical.
Distance Progression Intermediate Rush ball at varying distances from target: 5, 7, 10, 15 yards. Practice at each distance, noting which you're most accurate at. Don't practice rushing to exact hoop position — practice rushing to where you need for the approach. Calibrate for your lawn speed.
Trimmer's insight: "Note that hitting range of only 5 yards means approach rushes are most valuable."

Croquet Stroke Drills

Source: Trimmer p. 192

Drill Setup & Execution Key Points
Three-Variable Calibration Intermediate From near Hoop 1, aim to send pioneer to Hoop 3 whilst going a yard in front of Hoop 2.
  • Attempt the stroke
  • Assess: Angle correct? Ratio correct? Power correct?
  • Adjust ONE variable
  • By 4th attempt, achieve very good result
 The three measurable outcomes: angle, relative distance (ratio), and overall power. Use additional balls if possible — this drill requires walking.
Pioneer Placement Intermediate The fundamental break-building stroke sequence:
  • At H1: send to H3, self to H2 approach
  • At H2: send to H4, self to H3 approach
  • At H3: send to H5, self to H4 approach
  • Continue through all hoops
 This is the workhorse stroke of break play. Practice until automatic. Note accuracy of both balls.
Ratio Series Intermediate Practice each ratio type deliberately:
  • Stop shot (1:5): striker minimal, object 10 yards
  • Drive (1:3): object 9 yards, striker 3 yards
  • Half-roll (1:2): object 6 yards, striker 3 yards
  • Roll (1:1): both balls 5 yards
  • Pass-roll: striker 6 yards, object 4 yards
 Adjust stance, grip position, and swing angle for each ratio type.

Trimmer's Technique Checklist

Before each stroke, verify:

  • Are you balanced at end of stroke?
  • Shoulders free, grounded in legs?
  • Lower hand at same point on mallet shaft?
  • Grip tightness consistent?
  • Mallet direction selected BEFORE the stroke?

If getting inconsistent results, one of these is usually the culprit.

Hoop Running Drills

Source: Trimmer pp. 192-194, 204

"Controlled hoop running is the skill that is least well recognised by players at the top level."
Drill Setup & Execution Key Points
Controlled Hoop Running Intermediate Ball 1 yard in front of hoop. Place marker 2 yards behind hoop (representing a ball needing to be rushed).
  • Run hoop
  • Ball MUST stop before the marker
  • If ball passes marker, you've lost the rush
  • Repeat 10 times, track success rate
 Target: 8/10 hoops with ball stopping before marker. "Practice as though the line represents a ball needing to be rushed forward."
Wire-Free Visualisation Advanced Ball in front of hoop at any angle.
  • Imagine NO wire exists
  • Decide: if no wire, where should ball stop?
  • Run hoop at that pace
  • Note where ball actually stops
  • Adjust until intention matches result
 This teaches true control rather than relying on wire. Over time, learn how far you should try to run if no wire were taken.
Close Approach Advantage Intermediate Approach hoop from in front with croquet ball to send.
  • Set up approach from 3 yards — note result
  • Now set up from 2 yards — note result
  • Compare: closer = easier hoop + longer send
 From break hygiene: approaching closer than normal gives MORE hoop control AND sends other ball further. Find your optimal approach distance.
Why hoop running is hard: (1) Results are more stochastic due to tiny differences in ball-hoop contact; (2) Considerable mental discipline required to treat practice as equivalent to a real break.

Combination Practice Routines

Source: Trimmer pp. 194-197

Trimmer emphasises that isolated skill practice has limitations — mental aspects of each stroke connect to the next stroke. These combination routines maintain motivation through realistic play.

Drill Setup & Execution Key Points
Corner IV Routine Intermediate One ball in Corner IV, one ball a yard north for croquet.
  • Take croquet from ball in Corner IV
  • Rush toward Hoop 1
  • Run hoop with control
  • Take croquet, rush to Hoop 2
  • Continue as far as possible
 Main combination practice routine. Combines all three key skills in realistic game sequence. How many hoops can you make?
Rob Fulford 2-Ball Break Advanced One ball in Corner IV, croquet from ball a yard north. NO 4th ball on lawn.
  • Take croquet, rush toward H1
  • Run H1 with extreme control
  • Rush back toward corner
  • Dig ball out a little further
  • Rush to H2 and continue
 Developed to work on generating 3-ball breaks from 2-ball situations. Extremely stretching. Introduces difficult choices like whether to take significant wire.
Reg Bamford H1-H3 Challenge Expert Same setup as Fulford drill. But only make H1 and H3 (skipping H2).
  • Make Hoop 1 with control
  • Rush toward H3 (not H2!)
  • Make H3
  • Rush back toward H1
  • Repeat: H1, H3, H1, H3...
 Even harder than Fulford's. Every rush is long, every approach must be sound. "Player must be very good to consistently make more than 3 hoops. How many can you make?"
Chris Clarke Peel Position Advanced Rush to H1 from near corner, pioneer at H2 (using U, R, Y, K).
  • Playing U, rush R to H1
  • Run hoop getting peel position
  • Send K to 2-back via croquet
  • Place R 2-3 yards away, send Y to 1-back
  • Continue through H3, H4
 Works on controlled hoop running, judging pull, and large croquet strokes. Practice "escape ball" from 2-3 feet.
12-Point Tracking Advanced Full 4-ball break setup. Score each hoop:
  • 3 = perfect position
  • 2 = good position
  • 1 = acceptable
  • 0 = poor
Track total through 12 hoops. Note which hoops/positions score lowest. 		Reveals where your break building is weakest. If H5-H6 always scores low, that's your focus area. Target: 30+ points (avg 2.5 per hoop).
Stephen Mulliner Repetition Intermediate Choose ONE specific position/stroke.
  • Practice that exact position 10 times
  • Record results
  • Repeat same position next session
  • Track improvement over days/weeks
 Mulliner's approach: same positions across many days, no practice on matchdays. Pure repetition builds muscle memory. Different from match-play practice.

Tactical Exercises

Source: Trimmer pp. 198-205

"Becoming a better tactician requires developing understanding of several aspects: knowledge of your own abilities, knowledge of opponent's abilities, and understanding how to make balanced decisions given data from both."
Exercise Method Key Points
Fine-Tuning Leaves Intermediate
  • Set up initial leave position
  • Ask: what SHOULD opponent do?
  • Modify to make that option less attractive
  • Repeat until no clear best option exists
  • That's your optimal leave
 Treat opponent as "good robot" who does what they should. Then tweak position to make their choice harder. Use Trimmer's flowchart: Make own outcome guaranteed → Evaluate opponent response → Make opponent's outcome less worthwhile.
Option-Spotting Intermediate
  • Place 4 balls in realistic positions
  • List ALL possible lines of play
  • For each option, think through consequences
  • Tweak ONE ball position slightly
  • How does that change the options?
 "Think about a few situations DEEPLY rather than many shallowly." Before you can fine-tune a leave, you need to SPOT the options.
Opening Competition Intermediate Two players, standard opening positions.
  • Player A places Turn 1 ball
  • Player B responds (Turn 2)
  • Continue alternating
  • First to 3 hoops + established break wins
  • Switch who goes first each round
 "By playing numerous openings back-to-back, mind gets focused on how long tice should be laid, and when best to shoot at tice." Play best of 7 rounds.
Break Hygiene Audit Intermediate During any break practice, at each hoop ask:
  • Where did I send the pioneer?
  • Which side of pivot did I land?
  • Where did I rush the pivot?
  • Was there a better choice?
  • Would doubling-up have helped?
 Break hygiene comes more from TACTICAL choices than accuracy. "The decision of how firmly to run a hoop is TACTICAL — trades short-term success vs future break prospects."
On Break Hygiene: "In a delayed TP, when running 3-back with other balls waiting near penult for the second peel, gaining a forward rush pays huge dividends and often makes the difference between finishing or not for players with handicap of -1 or -1.5."

Mental & Consistency Practice

Source: Trimmer pp. 206-209

The Yerkes-Dodson Law

Performance follows an inverted U-shape with stress:

  • Too relaxed = poor performance
  • Optimal stress = peak performance
  • Too stressed = poor performance
Exercise Method Key Points
Pressure Practice All Levels Before any practice stroke:
  • Imagine playing in a final
  • Crowd surrounding you, watching
  • Thousand eyes on this stroke
  • Will it be good?
  • Execute stroke under this mental state
 This builds match readiness. "Real matches feel less stressful after pressure practice." Practice can be modified to help correct for needing to perform under pressure.
Stress Calibration Intermediate
  • Hit 5 strokes while completely relaxed
  • Hit 5 strokes with moderate focus
  • Hit 5 strokes with intense concentration
  • Note accuracy at each level
  • Which state produced best results?
 Find YOUR optimal arousal level — it's different for everyone. Some play best slightly relaxed, others need intensity. Your optimal level may surprise you.
Croquet Stroke Points Game Intermediate Two players, markers placed on lawn.
  • Player A lays out markers
  • Both attempt same stroke
  • Closest ball to marker wins 1 point
  • Nominate a "priority ball" worth 2 points
  • First to 10 points wins
 Creates real stakes to simulate match conditions. Each player gets couple of practice attempts before the measured attempt for points.
Consistency Check Beginner
  • Set up exact same stroke 10 times
  • Execute each identically
  • Film yourself or have partner watch
  • Are your mechanics identical each time?
  • Identify and eliminate variation
 Trimmer contrasts Ian Burridge (complex mechanics) with Reg Bamford (simple, consistent). "Bamford's style is preferred because so little can go wrong with it." Simplify your mechanics.
"Good practice requires a focus on learning how to focus your mind with short practice sessions, then building from there. Don't start 'too much', straight off the bat."
